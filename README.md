# "What Were They Thinking?" Using Open-Text Responses to Validate Constructs in Survey Experiments

**Steven Denney** (Leiden University)

**Last updated:** 17 April 2026

---

Conjoint experiments are the dominant survey design in political science, yet they rest on an assumption that is rarely tested directly: that respondents reason about profile attributes in the way the construct of interest specifies. Open-text questions are already familiar as basic manipulation checks, but they can do much more. This paper argues they should be taken seriously as evidence about construct validity and experimental design, and it develops a framework that combines structural topic modeling (STM) for unsupervised discovery with large language model (LLM) classification for theory-driven confirmation.

The framework is demonstrated across three conjoint experiments. A primary application to immigrant naturalization in South Korea and Taiwan shows that a social integration cue shifts respondent reasoning from economic and practical criteria toward civic engagement, with effects of 7-18 percentage points across both methods and six LLM classifiers (Fleiss' kappa = 0.745). A second application, a standard conjoint on immigrant admission without within-attribute randomization, shows the framework transfers to the more common design and can refine constructs that an earlier analysis grouped too coarsely. A third application, a conjoint on attitudes toward North Korean migrant entrepreneurship for which expert human-coded classifications are available, benchmarks LLM classifications against that ground truth and shows that iterative codebook refinement produces substantial LLM-human agreement. Token-level log-probabilities extracted from the classification step measure model uncertainty; they predict when models will disagree but reveal that all models are overconfident relative to human coding.
